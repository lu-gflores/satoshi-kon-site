Renowned Works of Satoshi Kon

Satoshi Kon

1963 - 2010

Satoshi Kon attended Musahino Art University in Tokyo where he studied graphic design. He started his career as a managa artist and editor for Young Magazine and later made his debut with a short manga Toriko. Kon soon became an assistant to Katsuhior Otomo, the creator of the renowed anime Akira and went on to collaborate with several other animated films like Memories.

During this partnership, he continued his work in manga, creating Opus that ran between 1995 and 1996, up until 1997 where he began his directorial debut in Perfect Blue.

Critically Acclaimed Works

Perfect Blue

A young member of a popular Japananese idol group decides to retire from music to pursue acting. However, her perception of reality becomes twisted as she becomes a target of an obsessive fan and her past begins to haunt her.

Millennium Actress

A renowned actress retires at the pinnacle of her career and secludes herself from the public. A director decides to produce a documentary about her life. As she begins her story, reality and cinema blends in as the characters she acted as span in the movies from distant past to the future.

Tokyo Godfathers

On Christmas eve three homeless people, an alcoholic-gambiling-addict, a transgender woman and former drag queen, and a runaway teen girl, find a baby while searching through garbage and take it upon themselves to return the baby to the parents. Little did they know of what adventure it would bring them.

Paprika

In an not-so distant future, a revolutionary device called the 'DC Mini' acts as a dream detective which allows users to enter other people's dreams and explore their unconscious thoughts. However, before authuorization of use is granted by the government, a prototype is stolen and it's misuse could be devastating on the individual. A scientist decides to enter the dream under an alter-ego in attempts to find the perpetrator and stop their plot.

Critics

Kon's four adventurous anime features...established his reputation one of the world's pre-emininent avant-pop filmmakers. Given his interest in the possibilities of cyberspace and the nature of media, Kon's films have as much in common with the work of David Cronenberg or Oliver Assasys as with of Hayao Miyazaki. J. Hoberman - NY Times
Tokyo Godfathers plays out as a drama but it's enriched with perfectly calibrated comic relief that deftly livens up the somber story. It's a fable of serendiptious encounters, of strokes of luck, which counterbalance both the harshness of life and the demons the three main characters battle. Ren Scateni - Hyperallergic
Paprika is a creative, multi-layered feature, blending science-fiction, thriller and psychological drama. As with his other features, director Satoshi Kon provied his skills in terms of visual and storytelling, discussing themes like perception and the way reality and dreams may collide with each other. Rouven Linnarz - Asian Movie Pulse
